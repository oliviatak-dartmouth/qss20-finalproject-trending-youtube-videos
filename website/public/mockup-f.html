01
The Question

Does emotional content go viral? It depends where you look.

A 2012 study by Berger and Milkman found that emotionally arousing articles spread further online. But YouTube reaches audiences mostly through its recommendation algorithm, which optimises for watch time rather than social sharing. This project tests whether the same relationship holds.

prior research predicts —

High-arousal titles spread more

Studies on NYT articles and Twitter found emotional content performs better. Arousal activates people, and activated people share.

VS
this study found —

Neutral titles win on YouTube

The algorithm rewards watch time, not clicks. Neutral, descriptive titles may convert attention into sustained viewing more reliably.

02
The Data

A full year of US trending videos, labelled by sentiment.

Each of the 12,176 titles was scored using VADER sentiment analysis, giving a compound score from −1 to +1. From that, each title got a label (positive, neutral, or negative) and an arousal score measuring emotional intensity regardless of direction.

54.9% Neutral

No detectable emotional signal. Over half of trending titles fell here — many are keyword-heavy or non-English.

22.8% Positive

Compound score above 0.05. Most common in Entertainment, Comedy, and Music.

22.3% Negative

Compound score below −0.05. Highest share in News and Nonprofits categories.
