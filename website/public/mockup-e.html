The Question

Does emotional content go viral? It depends where you look.

A 2012 study by Berger and Milkman found that emotionally arousing articles spread further online. High-arousal emotions like awe, anger, and anxiety drove sharing. But YouTube reaches audiences mostly through its recommendation algorithm, which optimises for watch time rather than social sharing. This project tests whether the same relationship holds.

Prior research predicts

High-arousal titles spread more

Emotional content activates viewers and motivates sharing. Studies on NYT articles and Twitter both pointed this way.

VS
This study found

Neutral titles win on YouTube

The algorithm rewards watch time, not clicks. Neutral, descriptive titles may convert attention to sustained viewing more reliably.

02
The Data

A full year of US trending videos, labelled by sentiment.

Each title was scored using VADER sentiment analysis. The resulting compound score was used to assign a label — positive, neutral, or negative — and an arousal score capturing emotional intensity regardless of direction.

54.9% Neutral

No detectable emotional signal. Over half of all trending titles fell here.

22.8% Positive

Compound score above 0.05. Common in Entertainment and Comedy.

22.3% Negative

Compound score below −0.05. Highest share in News and Politics.
